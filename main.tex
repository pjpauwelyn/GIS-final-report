\section{Introduction}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Coffee is one of the most widely consumed beverages in the world, with hundreds of billions of cups enjoyed each year. It is produced from the roasted seeds of plants belonging to the \textit{Coffea} genus, of which \textit{Coffea arabica} is the most commonly used species, accounting for roughly 70\% of global production. In its natural environment, \textit{C.~arabica} grows in tropical highland regions, where it is typically found in humid forest ecosystems. Originally native to East Africa --- particularly Ethiopia and South Sudan --- the species has since been widely introduced to suitable regions across the globe \citep{PlantskewArabica}.

\subsection{Climatic Requirements of \textit{Coffea arabica}}

\textit{Coffea arabica} is adapted to tropical highland climates with moderate, stable temperatures. Optimal temperature for mature plant growth and fruit development is generally cited at 18--21\,°C, with acceptable performance up to approximately 24\,°C \citep{Scott2015}. Above these thresholds, fruit quality declines and productivity drops; above 25\,°C desiccation of the cherry accelerates \citep{Scott2015}. Prolonged exposure beyond 30\,°C inhibits growth, while temperatures below 18\,°C suppress development.

Temperature sensitivity is also phenologically stage-dependent. Germination is optimal around 30--32\,°C, reducing the germination window to approximately three weeks; at 17\,°C this extends to three months, and above 35\,°C germination is inhibited entirely \citep{Tummakate1991}. Bud initiation similarly requires brief exposure to elevated temperatures. This means that even under a warming scenario, interannual temperature variability matters as much as mean temperature shift.

\textit{C.~arabica} requires annual rainfall in the range of 1{,}100--1{,}800\,mm \citep{Alves2020}, with a critical 2--4-month dry spell needed to stimulate uniform flowering \citep{DaMatta2006}. Effective rainfall depends strongly on soil water-holding capacity, atmospheric humidity, cloud cover, and cultivation method. Agroforestry systems, for example, reduce direct insolation and evapotranspiration, effectively lowering the rainfall threshold compared to open monoculture \citep{GrandezAlberca2025}.

\subsection{Soil Requirements}

According to \citet{DaMatta2006}, \textit{C.~arabica} performs best in deep, well-drained soils with good structural properties that permit adequate root penetration and water movement. Soils with high organic matter content are favourable because they support nutrient availability and water retention without causing waterlogging. Slightly acidic conditions promote optimal nutrient uptake. In the Hawaiian context, Andisols --- derived from volcanic ash and characterised by high organic matter and excellent drainage --- and Hualalai Volcanics and Kau Basalt geological units are among the most suitable substrates \citep{DaMatta2006}.

The Government of Hawai\okina i aims to improve decision-making efficiency regarding agricultural land use by obtaining spatially explicit projections of coffee suitability under current and future climate conditions. This study was commissioned to: (i) identify the most suitable locations for \textit{C.~arabica} cultivation on the Island of Hawai\okina i under current climate; (ii) assess how suitability shifts under three CMIP6 climate scenarios (SSP1-2.6, SSP3-7.0, and SSP5-8.5) projected to mid-century; and (iii) evaluate spatial trends in available suitable land and outline broad mitigation considerations.

\subsection{Climate Change Pressures}

\textit{C.~arabica} plantations are already experiencing climate-driven stressors worldwide \citep{DellaPeruta2025}. Studies in Brazil \citep{deFreitas2024} and South America more broadly \citep{GrandezAlberca2025} document declining yields and contracting viable areas. Pollinator communities --- essential for consistent yields --- are also projected to decline or shift in range under warming scenarios \citep{Imbach2017}, adding an additional layer of uncertainty beyond the direct climatic envelope. Hawai\okina i is not immune to these pressures, and understanding how temperature and rainfall shifts interact with local soil and land-use constraints is a prerequisite for sound agricultural planning.

\section{Methodology}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The analysis follows a multi-criteria suitability modelling approach implemented in ArcGIS Pro. All datasets were standardised to a common coordinate reference system (\placeholder{specify CRS --- e.g.\ NAD83 / Hawaii zone 3 or WGS84 UTM 5N}), spatial resolution (\placeholder{specify cell size, e.g.\ 250\,m}), and extent (Island of Hawai\okina i). Individual factor layers were reclassified to a 1--3 suitability scale (1\,=\,unsuitable, 2\,=\,suboptimal, 3\,=\,optimal) or a binary / categorical scale where appropriate. The factor layers were then combined using the ArcGIS Suitability Modeler with weights assigned according to the relative importance of each criterion for \textit{C.~arabica} cultivation.

\subsection{Climate Scenarios}

Three Shared Socio-economic Pathway (SSP) scenarios were used to represent the range of plausible mid-century climate futures:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{SSP1-2.6} --- optimistic scenario; strong mitigation, global warming limited to approximately 1.5--2\,°C above pre-industrial by 2100.
  \item \textbf{SSP3-7.0} --- intermediate scenario; regional rivalry, moderate mitigation, warming of approximately 2--3\,°C by 2100.
  \item \textbf{SSP5-8.5} --- pessimistic scenario; fossil-fuel-dominated development, warming exceeding 4\,°C by 2100.
\end{itemize}

Temperature and precipitation rasters for each scenario were sourced from the CHELSA dataset (\placeholder{cite CHELSA version and time slice, e.g.\ CHELSA v2.1, 2041--2070 mean}) and processed identically to the current-climate layers (see Section~\ref{sec:dam}).

\subsection{Water and Irrigation Considerations}

The growing of \textit{C.~arabica} under conventional plantation management has high irrigation requirements, supplementing what precipitation alone provides \citep{DaMatta2006}. Groundwater recharge declines when land cover shifts from native forest to agriculture, further stressing freshwater availability on Hawai\okina i. A census projection analysis incorporating expected water consumption, irrigation demand, and freshwater availability metrics under different land-use scenarios would be required to fully assess the hydrological sustainability of expanding coffee production \citep{DellaPeruta2025}.

\subsection{Pollinator Risk}

There is evidence that pollinators --- essential for consistent \textit{C.~arabica} yields --- may decline or shift their range under climate change. \citet{Imbach2017} project serious pollinator declines in South American coffee-producing regions; analogous risks may apply in Hawai\okina i. This uncertainty should be factored into any planning assessment.

\subsection{Agroforestry as a Mitigation Strategy}

Agroforestry systems have shown significant increases in biodiversity, insect pollinator populations, and land-use water retention compared to open monoculture \citep{GrandezAlberca2025}. Implementing shade-grown or mixed systems could buffer the direct climatic stressors identified in this analysis. Financial incentives, such as those demonstrated in Honduras where local governments stimulate uptake through direct funding and regulated price premiums for sustainably certified coffee, provide a viable policy mechanism \citep{QuinonezCamarillo2025}.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
  \item Suitability scores are derived from a static snapshot of projected mid-century climate and do not capture interannual variability or extreme events.
  \item The analysis does not include a hydrological model; water availability is treated only as a function of precipitation suitability.
